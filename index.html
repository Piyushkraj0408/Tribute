Netaji Subhash Chandra Bose

Netaji Subhash Chandra Bose (1897-1945?) was a fearless freedom fighter and nationalist leader who played a vital role in India's struggle for independence. Born in Cuttack, Odisha, he left the Indian Civil Services to join the freedom movement, later becoming President of the Indian National Congress in 1938. Differing from Gandhi's non-violence, Bose believed in armed resistance and formed the Forward Bloc. During World War II, he organized the Indian National Army (INA) with the slogan “Give me blood, and I will give you freedom!” and inspired millions with “Jai Hind.” Though he reportedly died in a plane crash in 1945, his death remains mysterious. His courage, leadership, and vision of a strong, independent India make him one of the most respected heroes of the nation.

A close associate of Mahatma Gandhi, Patel also organized successful non-violent civil disobedience campaigns against the British Raj and established the Indian Administrative Service.

Rani Lakshmi Bai of Jhansi (born Manikarnika Tambe in 1828) is remembered as one of the most iconic heroines of India’s struggle against British rule. Married to Maharaja Gangadhar Rao of Jhansi, she became queen at a young age and took charge of her state after his death. When the British tried to annex Jhansi under the Doctrine of Lapse, she refused to give up her kingdom. In 1857, during the First War of Independence, she led her troops with extraordinary courage, riding into battle dressed as a warrior, sword in hand. She fought fearlessly against the British forces until she was martyred in 1858 at the age of just 29. Her legendary bravery and determination made her a symbol of resistance and inspired future generations to fight for freedom.
